1. Analysis

The Kiev→Kyiv transition represents one of the most dramatic and politically charged toponymic shifts in modern digital discourse, with our corpus analysis revealing an overall adoption rate of 66.3% across 30.6 million mentions—yet this aggregate figure masks profound disparities that illuminate the complex dynamics of language change in the digital age. The transformation began in earnest with Ukraine's October 2018 #KyivNotKiev campaign, gained institutional momentum through the US Board on Geographic Names (June 2019) and AP Stylebook adoption (August 2019), and accelerated dramatically following Russia's February 2022 invasion, with recent mentions showing 58.9% Kyiv usage in Google Trends data. However, the most striking finding emerges from the stark contrast between source types: while Wikipedia demonstrates 85.9% recent adoption and academic sources (OpenAlex) show 79.9%, news media captured in GDELT exhibits only 27.8% recent adoption despite 26.7% overall usage—suggesting that real-time news reporting, perhaps constrained by established editorial guidelines or source quotations, lags significantly behind encyclopedic and scholarly discourse in adopting politically sensitive toponymic changes.

The religious context analysis exposes perhaps the most revealing dimension of this transition, where denominational politics directly correlate with toponymic choice in ways that transcend mere linguistic preference. The Moscow Patriarchate maintains an overwhelming 96.6% preference for "Kiev" (1,795 vs 63 mentions), while the World Council of Churches shows 78.5% adoption of "Kyiv" and the Ecumenical Patriarchate of Constantinople uses "Kyiv" 60.9% of the time—percentages that mirror the broader ecclesiastical tensions surrounding Ukrainian Orthodox autocephaly and Russian Orthodox canonical claims. This pattern extends beyond religious discourse into academic domains, where our collocation analysis reveals "Kiev" clustering with historically-oriented terms ("soviet," "century," "history") while "Kyiv" associates with forward-looking research vocabulary ("urban," "national," "development," "species")—suggesting the Russian form has become linguistically fossilized in historical contexts even as contemporary usage shifts toward the Ukrainian spelling.

The LLM audit results present a fascinating recognition-recall gap that offers unprecedented insight into the temporal stratification of training data and the persistence of older toponymic forms in foundational language models. While 95% of models correctly identify "Kyiv" as the preferred form when presented with both options, only 87.5% spontaneously generate "Kyiv" in free recall tasks, and the overall Toponymic Alignment Score of 90.6% suggests that training corpora heavily weighted toward pre-2019 content continue to influence model behavior despite the dramatic real-world shift in institutional usage. This gap becomes particularly significant when considered alongside the Hunspell dictionary results, where "Kiev" remains accepted in en_US while "Kyiv" is rejected—indicating that foundational linguistic resources lag behind evolving geopolitical realities, potentially explaining why models trained on these resources exhibit residual preference for the Russian form even when explicitly programmed to recognize current preferred usage.

The domain-specific persistence patterns revealed in our context classification illuminate why certain spheres resist toponymic change more than others, with implications extending far beyond this single pair. Historical contexts show 62.8% retention of "Kiev," while war-related discourse demonstrates near-parity (51.6% vs 48.4%), suggesting that temporal distance from current events correlates with resistance to change—a pattern likely to characterize other politically motivated toponymic transitions. The bootstrap confidence interval (52.42-52.54%) confirms that this split is not merely sampling variance but represents genuine contested usage across domains, with religious contexts showing the most extreme polarization and academic contexts showing the strongest adoption of the Ukrainian form.

These findings suggest a predictive framework for other contested pairs: toponymic change adoption rates will likely correlate with institutional endorsement speed, source type (encyclopedic > academic > news > religious), temporal context (contemporary > historical), and underlying political affiliations of content creators. The Kiev→Kyiv case demonstrates that even with strong institutional support and geopolitical catalysts like military conflict, complete transition requires years rather than months, and certain contexts may indefinitely preserve older forms regardless of official preferences. For computational linguistics applications, this implies that toponym resolution systems must incorporate not just temporal layers but also source authority weighting and political context awareness to accurately reflect the complex reality of contested geographic nomenclature in digital discourse.
